One of the amateur radio operators selected by NASA to help track the [Artemis II mission](https://www.nasa.gov/mission/artemis-ii/) via RF is [posting regular updates to Bluesky](https://bsky.app/profile/coastal8049.bsky.social). Back in January, NASA [announced the volunteers](https://www.nasa.gov/technology/space-comms/nasa-selects-participants-to-track-artemis-ii-mission/) selected to help track the Orion spacecraft during the Artemis II mission and one of those volunteers was Scott Tilley (VE7TIL) who is also working with the University of New Brunswick in Canada which was also selected by NASA.

The [Vancouver Sun](https://vancouversun.com/) recently [published an article](https://vancouversun.com/news/bc-amateur-astronomer-tracks-artemis-ii-for-nasa) featuring Tilley and his progress tracking Orion so far: 

> The data will help NASA “identify ways to augment future moon and Mars mission support,” according to an agency statement. Or, as Tilley puts it, it’s a way to see if smaller stations that use technology such as his could be used on future moon missions, so they don’t have to task the larger, more costly primary systems.

Tilley tracked the Artemis I mission, [Mars probes from both China and NASA](https://www.arrl.org/news/british-columbia-radio-amateur-copies-signal-from-mars-orbiting-satellite), and many other satellites. You can follow his current progress with Artemis II on [Bluesky](https://bsky.app/profile/coastal8049.bsky.social) and learn more about his current focus on a new method of Doppler extraction.
